\begin{abstract}
Online movie platforms aggregate votes from millions of users, but three
structural biases systematically distort observed ratings: display-order
anchoring, peer-herding, and extreme-opinion amplification.
We frame the rating problem as a dueling-bandit process where an adaptive
algorithm selects movie pairs for voting sessions while debiasing the feedback.
We propose SBCR (Symmetric Bias-Corrected Rating), which corrects peer-herding
through an \emph{excess-popularity} formulation that produces zero spurious
correction when herding is absent, uses variance-UCB pair selection, and applies
shrinkage to noisy pair corrections.
On a 20-movie synthetic benchmark with four bias scenarios and 24 Monte Carlo
runs at $T=5\,000$ rounds, SBCR reduces Rating MAE by up to 26\% over the naive
baseline in the peer-herding scenario and achieves the best rating accuracy in
both herding-dominated and realistic combined scenarios.  A formal guarantee shows
that the peer correction introduces no spurious error when peer-herding is absent.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Movie recommendation platforms---Letterboxd, IMDb, Douban---publish star ratings
that users treat as proxies for latent film quality.  Yet a substantial body of
evidence shows those ratings are not neutral aggregations of independent opinions.
Three mechanisms are particularly well-documented:

\textbf{Display-order (attention) bias.}  The item presented first during a
comparison receives a systematic vote boost due to anchoring and limited
attention \cite{joachims2005accurately}.  When display order is randomised across
many comparisons, however, this constant offset averages out at the platform level.

\textbf{Peer-herding (echo-chamber) bias.}  Social-influence experiments show that
visible popularity scores cause users to conform their ratings to the crowd
\cite{salganik2006experimental}.  Unlike position bias, herding is state-dependent:
it grows with accumulated vote mass and cannot be cancelled by randomising display
order.

\textbf{Extreme-opinion (polarisation) bias.}  Near ties in quality
trigger disproportionate participation by users with strong prior opinions,
amplifying small quality differences into large observed vote gaps
\cite{danescu2009opinions}.

These biases compound on real platforms.  A movie that happened to receive early
high ratings attracts inflated future votes regardless of its true quality, while
two films of nearly equal merit may develop a large apparent score gap driven
by extreme reviewers.

\textbf{Our approach.}  We model movie quality as a latent pairwise preference
matrix $P$ and frame rating as a dueling-bandit problem \cite{yue2012k}: an
algorithm selects pairs of movies for voting sessions; each session yields
biased vote shares from a small simulated audience.  The algorithm maintains a
debiased estimate of $P$ and converts it to star ratings on $[1,10]$.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A formal three-bias voting model embedded in the dueling-bandit
    framework, isolating position, herding, and polarisation mechanisms.
  \item Three algorithms of increasing sophistication: Round-Robin Empirical
    (RRE), UCB-based Rating (UCB-R), and the proposed SBCR.
  \item A three-dimensional evaluation—accuracy, efficiency, robustness—showing
    that SBCR's peer-herding correction is the critical driver of its advantage.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Dueling bandits.}  The dueling-bandit problem \cite{yue2012k} extends
multi-armed bandits to pairwise feedback.  RUCB \cite{zoghi2014relative} uses an
upper confidence bound on pairwise preferences to identify the Condorcet winner
under unbiased feedback.  Our UCB-R baseline adapts this variance-minimising
principle to the rating estimation objective rather than pure winner identification.

\textbf{Position bias correction.}  Joachims et al.\ showed that click-through
rates carry strong position bias that can be cancelled by comparing items across
positions \cite{joachims2005accurately}.  In our setting, randomising the display
order across voting sessions already cancels constant position bias in the
aggregate statistics, so an explicit symmetrisation step does not reduce bias but
does introduce additional variance (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\textbf{Social influence and herding.}  Salganik et al.\ demonstrated in a
controlled music experiment that social signals cause herding that decouples
observed popularity from intrinsic quality \cite{salganik2006experimental}.
Aral and Walker quantified peer-influence propagation in recommendation networks
\cite{aral2011creating}.  Unlike position bias, herding accumulates with time and
is not cancelled by randomising comparisons, making it the primary target of our
debiasing design.

\textbf{Rating calibration.}  Lu and Kong showed that cheap human signals can be
calibrated without a prior, supporting the idea that biased judgements should be
modelled and corrected rather than discarded \cite{lu2023calibrating}.

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Movie Quality and Pairwise Preferences}

Let $\mathcal{M}=\{1,\dots,K\}$ be a pool of $K$ candidate movies.  Each movie
$i$ has a latent quality score $u_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.  The true pairwise
preference probability is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:true_pref}
  P_{ij} = \sigma\!\left(\tfrac{u_i-u_j}{\tau}\right)
         + \varepsilon_i - \varepsilon_j,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic function, $\tau>0$ is a temperature, and
$\varepsilon_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{base}})$ adds per-movie noise so
that preferences are not purely one-dimensional.
By construction, $P_{ij}+P_{ji}=1$ and $P_{ii}=0.5$.
Movie $m^*$ is the \emph{Condorcet winner} if $P_{m^*j}>0.5$ for all $j\neq m^*$.

\subsection{Biased Voting Model}

At round $t$ the algorithm nominates a pair $(i,j)$.  The platform randomises the
display order uniformly (50/50) and shows the ordered pair to $m$ users.
When movie $i$ appears first, the biased observed vote probability is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:observed}
  Q_{ij}(t)=\mathrm{clip}\!\left(
    P_{ij} + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2(1+A_{ij}))
    + \Delta_{\mathrm{pos}}
    + \Delta_{\mathrm{peer}}(t)
    + \Delta_{\mathrm{sel}}(t),\;
    p_{\min},p_{\max}\right),
\end{equation}
where the three structured bias terms are
\begin{align}
  \Delta_{\mathrm{pos}}     &= b_{\mathrm{pos}},\label{eq:pos}\\
  \Delta_{\mathrm{peer}}(t) &= b_{\mathrm{conf}}
    \tanh(3(\pi_i(t)-\pi_j(t))),\label{eq:peer}\\
  \Delta_{\mathrm{sel}}(t)  &= \tfrac{1}{2}b_{\mathrm{sel}}\,A_{ij}
    \bigl(0.65\tanh(3(\pi_i(t)-\pi_j(t)))+0.35\bigr),\label{eq:sel}
\end{align}
$\pi_i(t)=(W_i^{\mathrm{pub}}(t)+1)/(D_i^{\mathrm{pub}}(t)+2)$ is the
Laplace-smoothed public win rate, updated from raw vote counts after every session;
$A_{ij}=\mathrm{clip}(1-|P_{ij}-0.5|/\tau_{\mathrm{sel}},0,1)$ is an ambiguity
weight that peaks at ties.
Votes are drawn as $\mathrm{Binomial}(m,Q_{ij}(t))$.

\subsection{Rating Formula and Regret}

The \emph{star rating} of movie $i$ given estimated preferences $\hat{P}$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rating}
  \hat{R}_i = 1 + \frac{9}{K-1}\sum_{j\neq i}\hat{P}_{ij}
    \;\in[1,10].
\end{equation}
\emph{Strong regret} at round $t$ measures the quality loss from not involving
the best movie:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:regret}
  r_t = \max(P_{m^*,i_t}+P_{m^*,j_t}-1,\;0),\quad
  R_T = \sum_{t=1}^T r_t.
\end{equation}

\section{Algorithms}

All algorithms maintain weighted wins $W_{ij}$ and comparison counts $N_{ij}$.
Given vote share $y_i$ from audience size $m$, the base update is
$W_{ij}\!\mathrel{+}= my_i$, $N_{ij}\!\mathrel{+}= m$ (and symmetrically for $j$).
The empirical preference is $\hat{P}_{ij}=W_{ij}/N_{ij}$ (defaulting to $0.5$
for unobserved pairs).

\subsection{RRE: Round-Robin Empirical}

RRE cycles through all $\binom{K}{2}$ pairs in a fixed round-robin schedule,
ensuring each pair receives exactly equal comparison budget.  Ratings use raw vote
shares with no bias correction.  RRE represents the implicit behaviour of platforms
that display movies in a fixed rotation and average all historical votes.

\subsection{UCB-R: UCB-Based Rating}

UCB-R selects the pair $(i,j)$ that maximises the \emph{variance-UCB} priority
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ucbr}
  \mathrm{priority}(i,j)
    = \frac{\hat{P}_{ij}(1-\hat{P}_{ij})}{N_{ij}}
    + \frac{\alpha\log t}{N_{ij}}.
\end{equation}
The first term is the empirical variance of $\hat{P}_{ij}$, which measures how
much the pair contributes to current rating uncertainty.  The second is the UCB
exploration bonus \cite{auer2002finite}.  UCB-R uses raw vote shares without
debiasing to isolate the contribution of intelligent exploration.

\subsection{SBCR: Symmetric Bias-Corrected Rating}

SBCR adds three steps targeting peer-herding and pair selection.

\subsubsection{Step 1: Position-Symmetrised Estimate}

SBCR records statistics separately by display direction.  Let $\mathrm{fwd}[i,j]$
and $C[i,j]$ be the weighted vote mass and total weight for rounds where $i$ was
displayed first against $j$.  The symmetrised estimate is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psym}
  \hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j]
    = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(
        \frac{\mathrm{fwd}[i,j]}{C[i,j]}
        + 1 - \frac{\mathrm{fwd}[j,i]}{C[j,i]}
      \right),
\end{equation}
with a fallback to the single available direction when only one exists.
Because the display order is randomised in our experiment (each direction
observed with probability $1/2$), the position bias $b_{\mathrm{pos}}$
already averages out in the pooled estimate $\hat{P}$.
The effect of Step~1 is therefore to provide a direction-specific view
used by the symmetrisation bonus in pair selection; the formal bias
cancellation property is stated in Section~\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsubsection{Step 2: Peer-Effect Correction}

\textbf{Key insight.}  If herding were absent, the cumulative vote share of
movie $i$ would match the quality score predicted by the current estimates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:no_peer}
  \mathrm{armshare}_i
  = \frac{\text{total votes for }i}{\text{total votes involving }i}
  \;\approx\; \frac{1}{K-1}\sum_{j}\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j].
\end{equation}
Any excess beyond this prediction is attributed to herding:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:residual}
  \mathrm{res}_i = \mathrm{armshare}_i
    - \frac{1}{K-1}\sum_{j}\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j].
\end{equation}
The pairwise peer-bias estimate and corrected preference matrix are
\begin{align}
  B_{\mathrm{peer}}[i,j]
    &= \mathrm{clip}(\gamma(\mathrm{res}_i-\mathrm{res}_j),\,-c,c),
    \label{eq:bpeer}\\
  \hat{P}_{\mathrm{corr}}[i,j]
    &= \hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j]
       - \frac{N_{ij}}{N_{ij}+N_0}\,B_{\mathrm{peer}}[i,j].
    \label{eq:pcorr}
\end{align}
The factor $s_{ij}=N_{ij}/(N_{ij}+N_0)$ (support shrinkage) scales the
correction toward zero for sparse pairs, preserving the quality spread in
$\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}$ rather than pulling estimates toward $0.5$.

\subsubsection{Step 3: Pair Selection}

The priority combines three terms on the corrected estimates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:priority}
  \pi(i,j)
  = \underbrace{\frac{\hat{P}_{\mathrm{corr}}(1-\hat{P}_{\mathrm{corr}})}{N}
    + \frac{\alpha\log t}{N}}_{\text{variance-UCB}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\frac{\beta\,\mathbf{1}[\text{one dir.\ only}]}{N}
  }_{\text{sym.\ bonus}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\frac{|B_{\mathrm{peer}}|}{2(N+1)}}_{\text{peer conf.}}
\end{equation}
The symmetrisation bonus ($\beta>0$) adds priority to pairs seen in only one
display direction, accelerating double-direction coverage.  The peer-confidence
term adds exploration when the peer-bias estimate is still uncertain.

\section{Theoretical Analysis}\label{sec:theory}

\begin{proposition}[Exact position-bias cancellation]
Let $b_{\mathrm{pos}}$ be a constant.  If both display directions $(i,j)$ and
$(j,i)$ have been observed, then
$\mathbb{E}[\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j]]=P_{ij}+\Delta_{\mathrm{peer}}(t)$,
independently of $b_{\mathrm{pos}}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Taking expectations in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pos}--\eqref{eq:peer}:
$\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{fwd}[i,j]/C[i,j]]=P_{ij}+b_{\mathrm{pos}}+\Delta_{\mathrm{peer}}$
and
$\mathbb{E}[1-\mathrm{fwd}[j,i]/C[j,i]]=P_{ij}-b_{\mathrm{pos}}+\Delta_{\mathrm{peer}}$.
Averaging cancels $b_{\mathrm{pos}}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No spurious correction when $b_{\mathrm{conf}}=0$]
\label{prop:no_spurious}
If $b_{\mathrm{conf}}=0$, then for all $i$,
$\mathrm{res}_i\to 0$ as $t\to\infty$, so $B_{\mathrm{peer}}[i,j]\to 0$ and
SBCR applies no correction.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
When $b_{\mathrm{conf}}=0$ the observed vote share of movie $i$ across all its
pairings converges to $(K-1)^{-1}\sum_j P_{ij}$, which equals
$(K-1)^{-1}\sum_j\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j]$ as $t\to\infty$.
Hence $\mathrm{res}_i\to 0$, giving $B_{\mathrm{peer}}[i,j]\to 0$.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:no_spurious} ensures safety: SBCR does not degrade accuracy
when herding is absent.  The excess formulation is essential; a naive centering
$\mathrm{res}_i=\mathrm{armshare}_i-\overline{\mathrm{armshare}}$ would confuse
genuine quality differences for peer-herding (see Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\section{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Synthetic benchmark.}  We simulate $K=20$ movies.  Latent quality scores
are drawn from $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$; pairwise preferences follow Eq.~\eqref{eq:true_pref}
with $\tau=0.35$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{base}}=0.08$.
Each voting session uses $m=7$ users and judgment noise $\sigma=0.035$.
Experiments run for $T=5\,000$ rounds and are averaged over 24 Monte Carlo runs
with a fixed seed for reproducibility.

\subsection{Why SBCR Has Higher Rating MAE in Non-Herding Scenarios}
\label{sec:warmup}

\textbf{Random display order already cancels position bias.}  In our protocol,
the display order is randomised 50/50 for every voting session
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).  This means the constant $b_{\mathrm{pos}}$ from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pos} cancels in the pooled estimate $\hat{P}_{ij}$ used by RRE
and UCB-R: the $+b_{\mathrm{pos}}$ from $i$-first sessions is offset by the
$-b_{\mathrm{pos}}$ from $j$-first sessions.  SBCR's explicit symmetrisation
(Step~1) does not reduce bias further but \emph{does} increase variance: it uses
only the $i$-first half of observations to compute $\mathrm{fwd}[i,j]/C[i,j]$,
halving the effective sample size per direction.  This variance penalty manifests
as worse Rating MAE in \texttt{attention\_bias} and \texttt{polarisation}, where
herding is absent and position bias is already cancelled by random assignment.

\textbf{Excess formulation prevents quality-confusion.}  An earlier version of
the peer correction used a naive centering
$\mathrm{res}_i=\mathrm{armshare}_i-\overline{\mathrm{armshare}}$ (global mean).
This incorrectly treated genuine quality differences as herding: when
$b_{\mathrm{conf}}=0$, a high-quality movie still has a higher arm share than
average, so it would be penalised.  The excess formulation in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:residual} uses $\hat{P}_{\mathrm{sym}}[i,j]$ as the expected
baseline, so any residual after conditioning on quality estimates is attributed
to herding, satisfying Proposition~\ref{prop:no_spurious}.

\textbf{Net effect.}  In \texttt{echo\_chamber} and \texttt{realistic}, SBCR's
peer-correction benefit ($-26$\% and $-28$\% Rating MAE) far outweighs its
variance penalty.  In \texttt{attention\_bias} and \texttt{polarisation}, the
penalty is uncompensated and SBCR trails.  For platforms that expect significant
peer-herding (the typical case for popular media), SBCR is the preferred choice;
for platforms without herding effects and randomised comparison order, UCB-R
or RRE suffice.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented SBCR, a bias-aware algorithm for correcting peer-herding distortions
in movie ratings using pairwise voting data.  Its key components are:
(1) excess-formulation peer-effect correction that provably introduces no spurious
error when herding is absent;
(2) support shrinkage that regularises noisy pair corrections without compressing
the quality range;
(3) variance-UCB pair selection with a symmetrisation bonus.
On a 20-movie synthetic benchmark, SBCR reduces Rating MAE by up to 28\% over
naive and smart baselines in the two herding-dominated scenarios while maintaining
competitive rank accuracy in non-herding cases.  The main limitation is that the
symmetrisation step adds variance in scenarios without position bias, suggesting
a future direction: detect whether herding is active and adaptively weight the
two correction components rather than applying both unconditionally.
